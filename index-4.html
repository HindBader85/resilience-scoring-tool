The Formula

Five layers of resilience, weighted by their contribution to actual recovery.

10% High Availability
25% Storage Replication
20% Backup
10% Backup Replication
35% DR Readiness

Each layer either contributes its full weight (when present and operational) or zero (when absent). Sum across the five layers gives a per-service resilience score from 0% to 100%. Portfolio score is the arithmetic mean across services.

How to use

Three things to know before you start.

  1. Toggle each layer that is present and operational

    A layer counts only when it is fully implemented and would actually function in a real disaster. "In progress" or "partially configured" does not count. This is the most common misinterpretation and the one most likely to inflate scores.

  2. The score updates live

    No Save or Submit button. As you toggle layers, per-service scores recalculate and the portfolio score on the right updates immediately. Insights regenerate the same way.

  3. Your data stays in your browser

    Nothing is sent anywhere. Add, edit, or remove services freely. Closing the tab clears your input. The methodology is designed for sensitive infrastructure data and assumes nothing should leave your machine.

Methodology

The thinking behind the weights.

This methodology was developed to give telecom infrastructure teams a single, defensible number to track DR programme progress. Each weight reflects how much that layer contributes to actual recovery, not just to compliance.

What the score measures

Resilience scoring answers a single executive question: "if a critical service fails right now, how recoverable are we?" The score is not a measure of system uptime, not a measure of past performance, and not a SLA report. It is a forward-looking measure of current preparedness.

Five layers contribute to that preparedness, each weighted by how much it contributes to a real recovery scenario:

Layer Weight Rationale
High Availability 10% Mitigates in-component failure only (single node, single disk, single host). Important but preventive: it does nothing for site-level disasters.
Storage Replication 25% Enables near-zero RPO. Foundation for any meaningful active failover capability. Without this, recovery depends on backup restoration.
Backup 20% Last line of defense. Only layer that protects against logical corruption, ransomware, and propagated errors. Slower recovery than replication, but irreplaceable in worst-case scenarios.
Backup Replication 10% Protects backups themselves against site-level loss. Secondary tier in a model where storage replication is the primary off-site mechanism.
DR Readiness 35% Tested, validated failover capability. The only layer that proves the others actually work. Weighted highest because without it, the rest is theoretical.

Why these weights, not others

The weights reflect a clear philosophy: recovery beats prevention, tested capability beats untested capability, and active replication beats passive backup for RPO-sensitive workloads.

HA gets the lowest weight (10%) because it only handles in-site, in-component failures. That's important for everyday uptime but useless against disasters. DR Readiness gets the highest weight (35%) because no other layer matters if the failover has not been tested. The middle weights (Storage Replication 25%, Backup 20%, Backup Replication 10%) reflect their relative contribution to recovery time and recovery point objectives.

ISO alignment

Each layer maps to specific clauses in the ISO standards governing business continuity and IT disaster recovery:

  • ISO 22301 §8.3 Business continuity strategies: informs Storage Replication and DR Readiness weighting
  • ISO 22301 §8.4, §8.5 BCMS exercising and testing: directly underpins DR Readiness as the highest-weighted layer
  • ISO/IEC 27031 §7.3 Recovery strategies: anchors Storage Replication and Backup weighting
  • ISO/IEC 27031 §7.4 Incident response capability: anchors HA and Backup Replication
  • ISO/IEC 27031 §7.5 Testing and evaluation: reinforces DR Readiness as foundational
  • ISO/IEC 27001 A.12.3 Information backup: directly anchors Backup and Backup Replication

How to use it

For each service in scope, evaluate whether each of the five layers is present and operational. A layer counts only if it is fully implemented and would actually function in a real disaster. "In progress" or "partially configured" does not count. The score updates live as you toggle layers in the tool above.

The portfolio score is the arithmetic mean of per-service scores. For weighted portfolio views (by service criticality or business value), the methodology can be extended with per-service multipliers. Version 1.0 keeps the model simple by using flat averaging.

Service tiering

Services are classified into two tiers based on operational criticality. The classification informs where attention goes first: a Tier 1 service scoring below threshold demands immediate remediation, while the same score on a Tier 2 service can be addressed in a later phase.

Tier Definition Typical examples
Tier 1
Mission-critical		 Services whose unavailability causes immediate revenue loss, customer impact, or regulatory exposure. Recovery is a board-level concern. Billing platforms, customer-facing portals, CRM, charging systems, core network elements.
Tier 2
Critical supporting		 Services that enable Tier 1 operations but are not customer-facing themselves. Their failure degrades but does not immediately halt service delivery. Integration buses, internal analytics, call-center back-ends, middleware platforms.

Version 1.0 uses two tiers because that maps to most telecom operational environments and keeps the model communicable to executives. Organisations with a more granular classification (three or four tiers) can adopt the methodology by treating the third tier as informational rather than scored, or by extending the insights rules in future versions.

Interpreting the score

A resilience score is only useful when there is a shared understanding of what each range represents. RSI defines five interpretation bands that map score ranges to operational meaning and recommended posture. The thresholds align to the underlying weight math: each band corresponds to a meaningful structural change in which RSI layers are present.

Range Classification Operational meaning Recommended posture
0–25% Limited resilience The service is protected primarily by foundational controls. Recovery from site-level disruption is possible but requires extended time and incurs material data loss. Appropriate for non-essential workloads. Tier 1 services in this band warrant prioritised remediation.
25–50% Baseline resilience Multiple protection layers are in place. The service withstands routine failures, but active recovery capability is not yet established. Defensible as an interim state. Mission-critical services should have a documented path to active recovery.
50–70% Active resilience Replication or comparable active recovery mechanisms are in place. End-to-end recovery has not yet been validated under controlled conditions. The natural next milestone is validation through scheduled DR exercising.
70–85% Validated resilience DR readiness has been demonstrated. Recovery has been exercised and the capability is operational. Considered an acceptable steady state for most Tier 1 services. Continued exercising is required to maintain validity.
85–100% Mature resilience The service has comprehensive, validated, and exercised protection across all five RSI layers. Recovery within stated RTO and RPO objectives is achievable. The target state for mission-critical services. Sustained through periodic re-exercising and ongoing review.

The thresholds carry analytical meaning beyond their face value. Crossing 50% generally implies that a service has at least one form of active recovery in place, since the underlying weights make it mathematically unlikely to reach this band without it. Crossing 70% effectively requires DR Readiness to be present and tested, because DR Readiness alone is worth 35 points and is the only layer that proves the others actually function under disaster conditions. The bands therefore align to genuine operational transitions, not arbitrary thresholds.

Prior art and what makes this different

Weighted-sum resilience scoring is not novel as a category. Several established models exist, each calibrated for a different audience and infrastructure profile. Acknowledging them here makes clear what this methodology contributes that the others do not.

Related models in the field

Bryghtpath ISO 27031 Maturity Model assesses IT disaster recovery using a 0 to 5 COBIT maturity scale aligned to ISO 27031 clauses. It measures how mature each capability is, not whether the capability is present and weighted by its recovery contribution.

AWS Resilience Hub publishes a weighted-average formula across four components (Test coverage, Alarms coverage, SOP coverage, RTO/RPO compliance), with default weights 20/20/20/40. It is designed for cloud-native applications and measures AWS-recommended controls.

Recovery Maturity Models (including US Patent 9208006) use weighted sums to evaluate readiness for DR test execution. They are scoped to test-control workflows rather than to a layered infrastructure resilience view.

What is specific to this methodology:

  1. A layered breakdown calibrated for on-premise telecom infrastructure (HA, Storage Replication, Backup, Backup Replication, DR Readiness), not for cloud applications or maturity assessments.
  2. A percentage-of-presence model rather than a maturity-tier model, which is easier to communicate to executives ("we are at 43%") than ordinal scores ("we are at level 2.25 in three categories").
  3. Weights that align directly to phased rollout milestones: each milestone (storage replication, DR readiness) moves the score by a predictable amount. The formula and the programme plan move together.

RSI Methodology

The Resilience Scoring Index (RSI) is a weighted methodology that scores disaster recovery posture across five resilience layers. Each layer contributes its full weight to the score when present and operational, and zero when absent. The portfolio score is the arithmetic mean across services.

The Five Layers and Their Weights

Storage Replication 25% Enables near-zero RPO. Foundation for any meaningful active failover capability.
Backup 20% Last line of defense against logical corruption, ransomware, and propagated errors.
Backup Replication 10% Protects backups against site-level loss. Secondary tier to active storage replication.
DR Readiness 35% Tested, validated failover capability. Proves the other layers actually work in a disaster.

RSI weights are anchored to specific clauses in the ISO standards governing business continuity and IT disaster recovery:

  • ISO/IEC 27031 §7.3, §7.4, §7.5 Recovery, response, and testing capability
  • ISO/IEC 27001 A.12.3 Information backup

Tier 2 (critical supporting): services that enable Tier 1 operations but are not customer-facing themselves.
